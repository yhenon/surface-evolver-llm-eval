Surface Evolver Bench

How good are Large Language Models (LLMs) at writing complex physical simulations in a custom data format?

Results Github

Introduction

Surface Evolver is a tool released in 1992 (!) for modeling liquid surfaces. It is useful for tasks such as studying solder deposition on chips, modeling liquid fuel tanks or designing lab-on-a-chip networks.

To simulate a liquid, we must define a geometry using vertices, edges, faces and bodies using the custom datafile format. But it is not just a geometry or meshing task, as we must also encode the physics in Evolver's custom datafile language. Most importantly, for wetted liquid-solid boundaries, we do not represent the surface explicitly. Rather we leave a 'hole' in the mesh, and use Green's theorem to turn an area term into a boundary integral: \[ A = \frac{1}{2}\oint_{\partial A} \left(x\,dy - y\,dx\right). \] This means that for a liquid-solid surface we don't have to approximate it as a mesh, and deal with the complications of mesh refinement, equiangulation, and stretching or disappearing triangles. But we pay the cost in having to write a much more complex datafile, defining physics constraints due to the boundary integral.

Above: an example of a typical droplet of fluid on a flat surface, with the unmeshed 'hole' at the bottom of the droplet replace by a boundary integral.

What this eval measures

This eval tests LLMs capacity to:

  • Define complex 3D structures using vertices, edges and facets with minimal visual feedback.
  • Apply physics based constraints, like hydrophobic or hydrophilic surfaces, gravity terms, external forces and area minimization terms.
  • Correctly apply Green's theorem to transform area minimization constraints into boundary integrals.
  • Generalize from a few examples as there isn't a significant amount of web data.

Model grading:

  • No LLM-as-a-judge.
  • For each task, I carefully wrote a ground truth solution and simulated the converged liquid shape. For grading, the shape volume, area and energy is compared between the simulated solution and ground truth solution.
  • There are partial points for submitting a valid file, having a correct initial geometry and writing constraints correctly.

Results

Why another LLM benchmark?

There are already many benchmarks out there, which naturally poses the question of why create another yet another one.

For one, I wanted to. I thought it would be a good way to improve my understanding of the current capabilities of LLMs, which it absolutely did. So what did I learn?

  • You often hear "the harness is everything," and this is indeed true. The available tool calls, docs, and feedback loop make a huge difference for performance. My first versions had performance close to zero when docs were not accessible, the interaction with Surface Evolver was limited, etc
  • LLMs have a surprisingly strong understand of 3D geometry. I usually need to make a large sketch of the surface I am simulating, but they can seemingly deal with 3D structures with 20-30 vertices, tens of edges and faces in pure text.
  • LLMs are the real deal. There is a lot of debate over whether they are true intelligence, but clearly they can master a task that is challenging for many grad students from limited training data.
  • You do not have frontier intelligence at home (unless you live in a data center). This is unfortunate, but no small model performs well
