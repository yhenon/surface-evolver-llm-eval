Surface Evolver Bench

How good are Large Language Models (LLMs) at writing complex physical simulations in a custom data format?

Results Github

Introduction

Surface Evolver is a tool released in 1992 (!) for modeling liquid surfaces. It is useful for tasks such as studying solder deposition on chips, modeling liquid fuel tanks or designing lab-on-a-chip networks.

Simulating a liquid means defining a geometry (vertices, edges, faces and bodies) in Evolver's custom datafile language, and encoding the physics too. The key trick: for wetted liquid-solid boundaries we don't mesh the surface at all. We leave a 'hole' in the mesh and use Green's theorem to turn its area into a boundary integral: \[ A = \frac{1}{2}\oint_{\partial A} \left(x\,dy - y\,dx\right). \] This avoids mesh refinement, equiangulation, and stretching or disappearing triangles at the cost of a more complex datafile and hand-written constraints.

What this eval measures

This eval tests LLMs' capacity to:

  • Define complex 3D structures using vertices, edges and facets with minimal visual feedback.
  • Apply physics-based constraints, like hydrophobic or hydrophilic surfaces, gravity terms, external forces and area minimization terms.
  • Correctly apply Green's theorem to transform area minimization constraints into boundary integrals.
  • Generalize from sparse examples in a low-resource domain.

Model grading:

  • No LLM-as-a-judge.
  • For each task, I carefully wrote a ground truth solution and simulated the converged liquid shape. For grading, the shape’s volume, area, and energy are compared between the simulated solution and ground truth solution.
  • There are partial points for submitting a valid file, having a correct initial geometry and writing constraints correctly.

Results

Why another LLM benchmark?

There are already many benchmarks out there, which naturally poses the question of why create yet another one.

For one, I wanted to. I thought it would be a good way to improve my understanding of the current capabilities of LLMs, which it absolutely did. I also thought it did genuinely make a good benchmark, with some nice aspects like limited training data, and it is truly agentic with the iterative submissions of the datafile. So what did I learn?

  • You often hear "the harness is everything," and this is indeed true. The available tool calls, docs, and feedback loop make a huge difference for performance. My first versions had performance close to zero when docs were not accessible, the interaction with Surface Evolver was limited, etc.
  • LLMs have a surprisingly strong understanding of 3D geometry. I usually need to make a large sketch of the surface I am simulating, but they can seemingly deal with 3D structures with 20-30 vertices, tens of edges and faces in pure text.
  • LLMs are the real deal. There is a lot of debate over whether they are true intelligence, but clearly they can master a task that is challenging for many grad students from limited training data.
  • You do not have frontier intelligence at home (unless you live in a data center). This is unfortunate, but no small model performs that well.
  • OpenRouter is great for switching models with a unified API, but it seems too easy to run an open-source model through an unreliable provider with a misconfigured or heavily quantized model. I tried to run all models through the official provider only.
  • I was initially quite concerned with reward hacking but this did not happen. Maybe because the grading was kept somewhat opaque?
